# PR & Media — CEO Guide

Use this when the CEO needs to handle media inquiries, manage public reputation, respond to a crisis, write a press release, prepare for an interview, or manage the company's public narrative.

---

## Standard output format

**PR Summary — [Situation]**

**Headline:** [What is the PR situation in one sentence?]

**Urgency:** Immediate (hours) / Soon (24–48 hrs) / Planned (days to weeks)

**Risk level:** Low / Medium / High / Reputational threat

**Recommended response:** [Specific action — what to say, who says it, what channel, when]

**What NOT to do:** [The specific mistake to avoid in this situation]

## Crisis communication — step by step

When something goes wrong publicly (outage, bad press, employee incident, product failure):

### Within the first hour
1. **Acknowledge** — say something before the story is written for you. Even "We're aware and investigating" buys time and goodwill.
2. **Don't speculate** — only say what you know is true. "We believe..." and "It appears..." create liability.
3. **Assign a spokesperson** — one person speaks. Not multiple.
4. **Monitor** — who is talking about this and what are they saying?

### Within 24 hours
5. **Full statement** — what happened, what you did, what changes
6. **Direct outreach** — proactively contact affected customers before they contact press
7. **Internal alignment** — brief the team on what to say if asked (one message)

### Ongoing
8. **Follow through** — if you promised something, deliver it and communicate that you did
9. **Post-mortem** — what changed to prevent recurrence?

## Press release structure

Use this structure for any press announcement:

---

**FOR IMMEDIATE RELEASE**

**[COMPANY NAME] [ANNOUNCES / LAUNCHES / PARTNERS WITH / CLOSES] [WHAT]**

*[Subheadline: one sentence expanding on the headline]*

**[City, Date]** — [Company Name], [one-line company description], today announced [what happened].

[First paragraph: the news in plain language. What happened, why it matters, who it affects.]

[Second paragraph: quote from CEO or relevant executive. Make it human — not "We are pleased to announce." Say something real.]

[Third paragraph: context — why now, what this means for customers or the market]

[Fourth paragraph (optional): product or feature detail if relevant]

[Boilerplate]
About [Company Name]: [2–3 sentence company description, founding year, mission]

**Media Contact:**
[Name]
[Email]
[Phone]

---

### Press release rules
- Headline should be a news statement, not a marketing claim
- Lead with the most newsworthy fact, not background
- One idea per press release — don't bundle multiple announcements
- Quotes should sound like a human said them — not corporate speak
- Keep it under 500 words unless it's a major announcement

## Media interview preparation

### Before the interview
- What is the one message you want every reader/viewer to remember?
- What are the 3 supporting points that prove it?
- What is the hardest question you might be asked — and what's your answer?
- What is off-limits and how will you redirect if asked?

### The CEO message framework (for any interview)
**Headline:** [Your single most important message]
**Bridge phrase:** "What I think is really important here is..."
**Three proof points:** [Facts, customer stories, or numbers that back the headline]
**Close:** [Call to action or memorable closing line]

### Handling difficult questions
- **Hostile question:** Acknowledge, correct if needed, pivot to your message. Don't repeat the negative framing.
- **Speculative question:** "I can't speak to speculation, but what I can tell you is..."
- **Confidential topic:** "I'm not able to share details on that, but what I can say is..."
- **Competitor question:** Never disparage competitors. Redirect to your own strengths.

### What to avoid in interviews
- Never say "No comment" — it implies guilt. Redirect instead.
- Never go off the record with journalists you don't know well
- Never speculate about financials, headcount, or M&A activity
- Don't try to be funny — humor rarely translates in text

## Managing online reputation

### Review monitoring
- Check Google, Glassdoor, G2, Trustpilot, and industry review sites monthly
- Respond to negative reviews within 48 hours — professionally, not defensively
- Thank positive reviewers specifically (mention what they said)

### Glassdoor (employee reviews)
- A CEO rating below 70% is a yellow flag for candidates and investors
- Respond to negative reviews from a place of openness: "Thank you for this feedback. We take [specific issue] seriously and here's what we're doing..."
- Never respond defensively — it makes it worse

### Social media monitoring
- Set up Google Alerts for your company name and CEO name
- Monitor Twitter/X and LinkedIn for brand mentions
- Have a response template ready for common complaints:
  > "We're sorry to hear about your experience. Please reach out to [contact] directly and we'll make it right."

## Proactive PR — getting coverage

### What journalists actually want
- A real story: conflict, change, human impact, or surprising data
- Access to real customers (not just the CEO)
- Exclusive angles — don't pitch the same story to 10 publications at once
- Fast responses — if a journalist is working on a story and needs a quote in 2 hours, that's the window

### Types of stories that get covered
- **Data story:** Original research, survey, or unique data you have
- **Customer story:** A dramatic transformation or result a customer achieved
- **Funding announcement:** Raises above $1M typically get coverage in tech/business press
- **Industry insight:** CEO perspective on a trend (this builds thought leadership over time)
- **Controversy response:** Thoughtful response to an industry issue that's already in the news

### Thought leadership (long game)
- CEO posting on LinkedIn 2–3 times per week builds credibility over 6–12 months
- Speak at 1–2 industry events per year
- Contribute op-eds to industry publications — one good byline beats 10 press releases
- Build relationships with 3–5 journalists who cover your space before you need them

---

## PR red flags to surface immediately

- A current or former employee posting publicly about a workplace issue
- A customer posting a viral complaint about your product
- A competitor making false claims about your company
- A data breach being reported by press before you've responded
- A negative article being written — the journalist has reached out for comment
- An executive's social media post that is controversial or inflammatory
