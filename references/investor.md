# Investor Relations — CEO Guide

Use this when the CEO needs to communicate with investors or the board: investor updates, board meeting prep, fundraising materials, cap table questions, or managing investor relationships.

---

## How to handle specific investor situations

### Monthly investor update
- Keep it to one page or under 400 words
- Lead with the most important metric
- Be honest about challenges — hiding problems destroys trust
- Include a specific ask if you need help (introductions, advice, a reference call)
- Send on a consistent date every month

### Board meeting preparation
Structure the board package as:
1. **Executive summary** (1 page) — how is the business performing overall?
2. **Financial review** — P&L, cash, burn, forecast vs actuals
3. **Key metrics dashboard** — the 5–8 metrics the board cares most about
4. **Strategic update** — what decisions were made, what changed in thinking
5. **Risks and issues** — what keeps the CEO up at night (be direct)
6. **Asks for the board** — specific decisions or approvals needed
7. **Next quarter plan** — what the company will focus on

CEO rule: the board package should contain no surprises. If something significant happened — good or bad — board members should already know before the meeting.

### Fundraising narrative
The core fundraising story has 5 parts:
1. **Problem:** What painful problem exists in the market?
2. **Solution:** How does your product solve it better than alternatives?
3. **Traction:** What evidence proves this is working? (customers, revenue, growth rate)
4. **Market:** How big is the opportunity? (TAM — Total Addressable Market)
5. **Ask:** How much are you raising, what will you use it for, what will it achieve?

Keep the pitch deck to 10–12 slides. Lead with traction, not the problem.

### Responding to investor concerns
- Acknowledge the concern directly — don't deflect
- Share the data relevant to their concern
- Explain what you are doing about it
- Give a timeline for when they will see improvement
- Follow up in writing after the conversation

### Cap table question
- **Cap table** = the list of who owns what % of the company
- **Pre-money valuation** = company value before new investment
- **Post-money valuation** = company value after investment (pre-money + amount raised)
- **Dilution** = your ownership % goes down when new shares are issued
- **Pro-rata rights** = investor's right to maintain their % in future rounds
- **SAFE / Convertible note** = investment that converts to equity at a later round

## Common investor terms — plain language

| Term | What it means |
|------|--------------|
| ARR | Annual Recurring Revenue — annualized subscription revenue |
| MRR | Monthly Recurring Revenue — predictable monthly revenue |
| Burn Rate | How much cash the company spends per month (net) |
| Runway | Months until you run out of cash at current burn rate |
| Churn | Customers or revenue lost in a period |
| NRR | Net Revenue Retention — are existing customers growing in value? |
| LTV:CAC | Lifetime value vs cost to acquire — measures marketing efficiency |
| TAM | Total Addressable Market — the full size of the market opportunity |
| Series A/B/C | Labels for funding rounds (A = early growth, B = scaling, C = expansion) |
| Lead investor | The investor putting in the most money and leading the round terms |
| Term sheet | A document outlining the proposed terms of an investment |
| Valuation | What the company is worth (agreed upon during fundraising) |
| Dilution | Reduction in existing ownership % when new shares are issued |
| ESOP | Employee Stock Option Pool — shares reserved for team equity |

## Investor communication rules

1. **Monthly updates are non-negotiable.** Investors who feel informed become advocates. Investors who feel ignored become problems.
2. **Bad news early.** Always tell investors about a serious problem before they hear it elsewhere. Frame it with context and a plan.
3. **Specific asks get results.** "Let me know if you can help" gets nothing. "Can you introduce me to the Head of Procurement at [Company]?" gets results.
4. **Keep it short.** Investors get many updates. Under 400 words will be read. Over 800 words will be skimmed.
5. **Show progress against the last update.** Reference what you said you'd do last month and report back on it.

---

## Red flags to always surface

- Runway under 9 months with no fundraising process started
- A board member who hasn't engaged in 2+ months
- An investor who reached out with concerns you haven't responded to
- A promise made at the last board meeting that wasn't delivered
- Cap table issues (option pool not set up, incorrect ownership records)

## Live data — Google Drive + Gmail integration

### Google Drive — board and investor documents
When Google Drive is connected:
```
- Search for the most recent board deck (file names: "Board", "Board Update", "Board Meeting")
- Find the latest financial model or cap table
- Pull the most recent investor update document
- Find any fundraising materials (pitch deck, data room index)
```

**Auto-pull before board prep:**
> "I found your latest board deck from [date] in Drive. Pulling it now to help you prepare..."

**What to look for in Drive documents:**
- Date of most recent board deck → is it current or outdated?
- Whether the deck contains the metrics investors care about (ARR, burn, runway, NRR)
- Gap between last investor update and today → are investors informed?

### Gmail — investor communication signals
When Gmail is connected:
```
- Search for emails from known investor domains in the last 30 days
- Look for questions or requests from board members
- Find any investor update emails sent (check if last update was sent)
- Search for due diligence requests or intro emails from new investors
```

**Search terms to use:**
- `from:@sequoia.com OR from:@a16z.com` (adjust to your investors)
- `subject:board` — board-related emails
- `subject:investor update` — latest update sent
- `subject:due diligence` — active fundraising signals
