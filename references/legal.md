# Legal & Compliance — CEO Guide

Use this when the CEO needs to understand or act on legal matters: contracts, compliance requirements, employment law, IP protection, regulatory issues, or legal risks.

**Important:** This skill provides plain-language context to help the CEO ask better questions of their lawyer — it does not replace legal counsel. Always recommend involving a qualified attorney for anything with material legal consequences.

## Contract review — what to flag

When the CEO shares a contract to review, look for these red flags:

### Payment terms
- Net 60+ payment terms favor the client — push for Net 30 or upfront
- No late payment penalty means clients have no incentive to pay on time
- Auto-renewal clauses without notice periods can trap both parties

### Liability
- **Unlimited liability** — extremely dangerous. Always push for a liability cap (usually 1× contract value)
- **Indemnification clauses** — who pays if something goes wrong? Should be mutual, not one-sided
- **Consequential damages** — if uncapped, your company could owe more than the contract is worth

### Intellectual Property
- Who owns work product created during the engagement?
- If it's a client contract: ensure you retain rights to your core IP and tools
- If it's a vendor contract: ensure you own outputs built for you

### Termination
- Can either party terminate without cause? With how much notice?
- What happens to work in progress if terminated?
- Are there termination fees?

### Governing law and jurisdiction
- Which state/country's laws apply?
- Where would disputes be resolved?
- Arbitration vs. court — arbitration is usually faster and cheaper

### Non-compete / non-solicit
- How long does it last?
- How broad is the geographic scope?
- Does it prevent you from hiring the client's employees or vice versa?

## Employment law basics

### Hiring
- Employment contracts should clearly state: role, compensation, start date, at-will status (if applicable), IP assignment, confidentiality
- Non-competes are enforceable in some states/countries, unenforceable in others (California: unenforceable)
- Background checks require written consent in most jurisdictions

### Termination
- At-will employment (US): either party can terminate for any reason, with some exceptions
- Document performance issues before terminating — creates a record and reduces wrongful termination risk
- Severance is not legally required in most US states but is standard practice
- WARN Act (US): companies with 100+ employees must give 60 days notice for mass layoffs

### Equity and compensation
- Stock option grants require board approval and a formal grant document
- Vesting schedules (typically 4 years with 1-year cliff) must be documented
- Misclassifying employees as contractors creates significant tax and legal liability

### Key policies every company should have
- Employee handbook (at-will statement, harassment policy, PTO, conduct)
- IP assignment agreement (every employee and contractor should sign)
- NDA / confidentiality agreement (for employees, contractors, and partners)
- Data privacy policy (required if you handle customer data)

## Data privacy & compliance

### GDPR (EU customers)
- If you process data of EU residents, GDPR applies regardless of where your company is based
- Requirements: privacy policy, data processing agreements, right to erasure, data breach notification within 72 hours
- Penalties: up to 4% of global annual revenue or €20M, whichever is higher

### CCPA (California customers)
- Applies if you do business with California residents and meet certain revenue/data thresholds
- Requirements: privacy policy disclosures, opt-out of data sale, right to know what data you hold

### SOC 2 (common for B2B SaaS)
- Not a legal requirement but often demanded by enterprise customers
- Covers security, availability, processing integrity, confidentiality, and privacy
- Type I: point-in-time assessment. Type II: 6–12 month audit (more credible)

### HIPAA (US healthcare data)
- Required if you handle Protected Health Information (PHI)
- Requires Business Associate Agreements (BAAs) with all vendors who touch PHI
- Penalties for violations: $100–$50,000 per violation

## IP protection basics

### What to protect and how
| Asset | Protection |
|-------|-----------|
| Software code | Copyright (automatic) + trade secrets |
| Brand name / logo | Trademark (file with USPTO or local authority) |
| Unique process or formula | Trade secret (keep it confidential) |
| Novel invention | Patent (expensive, 2–3 years, but strong protection) |
| Content (blog, docs) | Copyright (automatic) |

### What to do now
1. File trademarks for your company name and product names in your primary markets
2. Ensure all employees and contractors sign IP assignment agreements
3. Document what makes your core product/process unique and treat it as a trade secret

## Common legal red flags to surface immediately

- A contract with unlimited liability exposure
- An employee or contractor who hasn't signed an NDA and IP assignment
- A data breach (even suspected) — legal obligation to notify in most jurisdictions
- A lawsuit threat or cease-and-desist letter — respond within the stated timeframe
- A key employee poached who signed a non-solicit agreement
- Operating in a new country without understanding local employment law
- Collecting customer data without a compliant privacy policy

## When to get a lawyer immediately

- Any litigation threat, lawsuit, or regulatory inquiry
- Fundraising (term sheet, cap table changes, investor agreements)
- Acquisitions or mergers
- Issuing equity to employees or investors
- Signing a contract worth more than $50K or with unusual liability terms
- Terminating a senior employee or a difficult HR situation
- Entering a new regulated industry or jurisdiction
