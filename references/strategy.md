# Strategy & OKRs — CEO Guide

Use this when the CEO needs help with strategic planning, OKR setting and tracking, quarterly reviews, annual strategy, or big-picture business decisions.

---

## OKR Framework

### What good OKRs look like

**Objectives** — qualitative, inspiring, time-bound direction
- Good: "Become the most trusted security platform for government buyers"
- Bad: "Grow revenue" (not inspiring) or "Do well" (not specific)

**Key Results** — quantitative, measurable, outcome-focused (not tasks)
- Good: "Achieve $2M ARR by end of Q3"
- Bad: "Launch the new dashboard" (that's a task, not a result)

**Rule of thumb:** 3 objectives per quarter, 2–4 key results per objective. No more.

---

### Setting OKRs with the CEO

Ask:
1. What does winning look like at the end of this quarter?
2. If we achieve nothing else, what are the 2–3 outcomes that matter most?
3. How will we know we've succeeded — what's measurable?

Then structure as:

**Objective:** [Qualitative goal]
**KR1:** [Metric] from [current] to [target] by [date]
**KR2:** [Metric] from [current] to [target] by [date]
**KR3:** [Metric] from [current] to [target] by [date]

---

### Grading OKRs

At end of quarter, score each KR 0.0–1.0:
- 1.0 = fully achieved
- 0.7 = strong progress, mostly achieved (this is the target — OKRs should be ambitious)
- 0.4–0.6 = partial progress
- 0.0–0.3 = missed or barely started

Average KR scores to get Objective score. Company health:
- 0.7–0.9 = strong — OKRs were appropriately ambitious
- 1.0 = too easy — set harder goals next quarter
- <0.4 = missed — investigate why and adjust approach

## Quarterly Planning Framework

### 6 weeks before quarter end — review and plan

**Week 1–2: Review current quarter**
- Grade all current OKRs
- What worked? What didn't? Why?
- What needs to carry over?

**Week 3–4: Draft next quarter OKRs**
- Start with company strategy (where are we going?)
- Then ask: what do we need to achieve THIS quarter to stay on track?
- Get input from all department heads

**Week 5–6: Align and finalize**
- Share draft OKRs with board / investors
- Cascade to teams (each team sets OKRs that ladder up to company OKRs)
- Finalize by end of first week of new quarter

## Annual Strategy Review

Structure the annual review as:

### 1. Where we are (30 min)
- Review the year's OKRs: what was achieved, what wasn't?
- Review key metrics vs. start of year
- What surprised us — positive and negative?

### 2. Where the market is (30 min)
- What changed in the competitive landscape this year?
- What do customers value now that they didn't a year ago?
- Where are the biggest opportunities we haven't captured?

### 3. Where we're going (60 min)
- What is the 3-year vision? (One sentence)
- What does Year 1 of that vision look like in numbers?
- What are the 3 strategic bets for next year?
- What do we need to stop doing to focus?

### 4. How we'll get there (30 min)
- What are the 3–5 company-level annual objectives?
- What resources (people, budget) need to shift?
- What does Q1 look like?

## CEO Decision Frameworks

### When facing a major strategic decision, use one of these:

**First Principles**
Break the problem to its fundamentals. Ask: "What do we know to be absolutely true about this?" Then rebuild the answer from scratch rather than from assumptions.

Use when: conventional wisdom seems wrong, or you're copying competitors without knowing why.

**80/20 Rule (Pareto)**
80% of results come from 20% of inputs. Ask: "Which 20% of our customers / products / activities drive 80% of our value?" Then do more of that and cut the rest.

Use when: deciding where to focus or what to cut.

**RICE Scoring** (for prioritization)
Score each option on: Reach × Impact × Confidence ÷ Effort
- Reach: How many customers affected?
- Impact: How much does it move the key metric? (1–3)
- Confidence: How sure are we? (% expressed as decimal)
- Effort: Person-months to deliver

Highest RICE score = highest priority.

Use when: prioritizing features, campaigns, or strategic initiatives.

**Pre-mortem**
Imagine it's 12 months from now and the plan failed. Ask: "What went wrong?" Then work backwards to identify the most likely failure modes and address them now.

Use when: making a high-stakes commitment (new market, major hire, large investment).

**Regret Minimization**
Ask: "When I'm 80, will I regret not doing this?" For big, irreversible decisions, this cuts through short-term fear.

Use when: a decision feels uncomfortable but strategically important.

## Red flags in strategy

- A company with no documented strategy (everyone is "just executing")
- OKRs that never get graded — they become decoration
- More than 5 company priorities — that means zero priorities
- Strategy that changes every quarter based on the last meeting
- No connection between OKRs and individual performance reviews
- A roadmap or plan that has never been said no to

## Live data — Notion + Google Sheets integration

### Notion — OKR tracking and strategic planning docs
When Notion is connected:
```
- Find the OKR database (look for pages named "OKRs", "Goals", "Objectives")
- Pull current quarter OKRs with progress status
- Find strategic planning documents (annual plan, 3-year vision, company roadmap)
- Pull decision log or ADR (Architecture/Any Decision Records) if maintained
- Find the team wiki for context on current priorities
```

**What to pull for OKR review:**
- All objectives for current quarter
- Key result progress (% complete or metric vs target)
- Owner for each KR
- Last updated date (stale KRs = no one is tracking)
- Notes on blockers or risks

### Google Sheets — metrics-based strategy tracking
Many companies track strategic metrics in Sheets (growth rates, market share, competitive data):
```
- Pull the company scorecard or metrics dashboard sheet
- Find the annual/quarterly targets sheet
- Pull the financial model (if shared) for planning context
- Find the OKR tracker if maintained in Sheets rather than Notion
```
