# M&A and Exit Planning — CEO Guide

Use this when the CEO needs to understand or act on mergers, acquisitions, strategic partnerships with equity components, fundraising with exit implications, or planning an exit (IPO, acquisition, or secondary sale).

## Exit options — plain language

### Acquisition (most common exit)
Another company buys you. They pay cash, stock, or a combination.
- **Strategic acquirer** — a larger company in your industry buys you for your product, customers, or team
- **Financial acquirer (PE)** — a private equity firm buys you as an investment, usually wants 3–5 year hold then resells
- **Acqui-hire** — they mostly want your team, less interested in the product

### IPO (Initial Public Offering)
You list your company on a stock exchange. The public can buy shares.
- Requires $50M+ ARR at minimum for most exchanges, typically $100M+
- Takes 12–18 months of preparation, significant legal and audit costs
- Suitable when: you want to raise large amounts of capital and have the scale to operate as a public company

### Secondary sale
Existing shareholders (founders, early investors) sell some shares to new investors without the company raising new money.
- Provides liquidity without a full exit
- Common at Series B–D when founders want to de-risk personal wealth

### Management Buyout (MBO)
The management team buys the company from investors or a parent company, often with PE backing.

## Valuation basics

### How companies are valued

**Revenue multiple (SaaS)**
- Early stage (< $5M ARR): 3–6x ARR
- Growth stage ($5M–$20M ARR): 6–12x ARR
- Scale stage ($20M+ ARR, fast growth): 10–20x ARR
- Public SaaS comps (2024): 5–10x ARR median

**EBITDA multiple (profitable businesses)**
- Services / agencies: 4–8x EBITDA
- SaaS (profitable): 8–15x EBITDA
- Manufacturing: 5–8x EBITDA

**Factors that increase your multiple:**
- High growth rate (>50% YoY)
- High NRR (>120%)
- Low churn (<1% monthly)
- Sticky enterprise contracts
- Proprietary technology or data
- Large TAM with clear path to $100M+

**Factors that decrease your multiple:**
- Revenue concentration (>30% from one customer)
- Declining growth
- High churn
- Key person dependency (business relies on founder)
- No defensible moat
- Messy cap table or legal issues

## The M&A process — step by step

### Phase 1: Preparation (3–6 months before)
1. **Clean up financials** — 3 years of audited or reviewed financials
2. **Fix legal issues** — IP assignments, contracts, employment agreements all in order
3. **Reduce key person risk** — document processes, distribute knowledge
4. **Build the data room** — organized folder of all due diligence documents
5. **Engage an advisor** — investment banker or M&A advisor (takes 3–7% success fee)

### Phase 2: Process (3–6 months)
1. **Teaser / CIM** — a 20–30 page document describing the business (created by your banker)
2. **Outreach** — banker contacts 30–50 potential buyers
3. **NDAs signed** — interested parties sign confidentiality agreements
4. **Management presentations** — 1-hour pitch to serious buyers
5. **IOI (Indication of Interest)** — non-binding offer with valuation range
6. **Select finalists** — narrow to 2–3 buyers for deeper diligence

### Phase 3: Negotiation and closing (2–4 months)
1. **LOI (Letter of Intent)** — binding exclusivity, non-binding price and terms
2. **Due diligence** — buyer digs into everything (legal, financial, technical, customer)
3. **Purchase agreement** — the binding legal contract
4. **Closing** — money moves, you sign over ownership

## Due diligence checklist — what buyers will ask for

**Financial**
- 3 years P&L, balance sheet, cash flow statements
- MRR/ARR breakdown by customer
- Cohort analysis (retention by month)
- Revenue recognition policy
- Outstanding invoices and accounts receivable aging

**Legal**
- Certificate of incorporation, cap table, shareholder agreements
- All customer contracts (especially enterprise)
- IP assignments from all employees and contractors
- Employment agreements for key people
- Any litigation, disputes, or regulatory issues

**Product / Technology**
- Architecture overview (non-technical description)
- Any open-source components and their licenses
- Security audit or penetration test results
- Uptime and reliability history

**Customers**
- Full customer list with ARR, start date, renewal date
- Top 10 customer contracts
- Customer concentration analysis
- Churn and expansion data

**Team**
- Org chart with roles and tenure
- Key person dependencies
- Equity table (who has options, how much, vested vs unvested)

## LOI — what to negotiate

The LOI is non-binding on price but sets the frame for everything after. Key terms:

| Term | What it means | CEO goal |
|------|--------------|----------|
| Purchase price | Total deal value | Maximize, including earn-out |
| Earn-out | Extra payment tied to hitting future targets | Minimize or eliminate — hard to hit post-acquisition |
| Exclusivity period | How long you can't talk to other buyers | 30–45 days max |
| Escrow / holdback | % held back for 12–18 months for indemnification | Minimize % and duration |
| Reps and warranties | Promises you make about the business | Limit scope and survival period |
| Employment terms | Your role and comp post-acquisition | Negotiate before signing LOI |

---

## Red flags in an M&A process

- A buyer who moves very slowly in due diligence — usually retrading price at the end
- An LOI with a 90+ day exclusivity period — gives them all the leverage
- Buyer asks for earn-outs tied to metrics you won't control post-acquisition
- No reference calls provided from other acquisitions they've made
- A buyer who wants the team but not the product — they'll shut it down
- Signing an LOI without engaging a lawyer — never do this

---

## CEO questions to ask any acquirer

1. What happens to my team after the acquisition?
2. How do you handle product integration — will our product survive?
3. Can I speak to 3 founders of companies you've acquired?
4. What does success look like for you in 2 years?
5. What would make you walk away from this deal?
