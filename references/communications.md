# Executive Communications — CEO Guide

Use this when the CEO needs to write or review any business communication: all-hands announcements, team emails, crisis responses, partner communications, press statements, or high-stakes messages.

---

## Communication type selector

| Situation | Format | Tone |
|-----------|--------|------|
| Company-wide announcement | All-hands email or meeting | Clear, direct, reassuring |
| Team update (one department) | Department email | Professional, specific |
| Crisis or bad news | Short internal email + optional external statement | Calm, honest, action-oriented |
| Investor / board communication | Structured update | Formal, numbers-first |
| Partner / vendor issue | Business email | Professional, firm, solution-focused |
| Customer-facing announcement | Email + optional in-app message | Customer-benefit first |
| Media / press inquiry | Official statement | Controlled, factual, no speculation |
| Executive response to complaint | Direct reply | Empathetic, decisive |

---

## All-hands announcement format

**Subject:** [Clear, specific — not "Important Update"]

---

Team,

**[What's changing — one sentence. Don't bury the lead.]**

[Why we made this decision — 2–3 sentences. Be honest about the reasoning.]

[What this means for you — what changes, what stays the same. Be concrete.]

[What happens next — timeline and next steps.]

[Optional: what you can do if you have questions — who to contact, when there will be a follow-up]

[Closing — one sentence. Forward-looking, not sentimental.]

[CEO Name]

---

**Rules for all-hands communication:**
- Lead with the news, not the context
- Use "we decided" not "the leadership team has determined" — own it
- Answer the question employees actually have: "what does this mean for me?"
- Don't over-explain or over-apologize — it signals uncertainty
- If it's bad news, say it directly. Softening bad news with corporate language makes it worse

## Crisis communication format

**Internal (to team):**

Subject: [What happened] — Update from [CEO Name]

Team,

[What happened — one paragraph, plain language, honest]

[What we are doing about it right now]

[What you should do / what changes for your work today]

I'll send another update by [time/date].

[CEO Name]

---

**External (to customers, if needed):**

Subject: [Issue] — What happened and what we're doing

[Acknowledge the issue directly — don't use passive language like "some customers may have experienced"]

[What caused it — brief, non-technical explanation]

[What we did to fix it and when it was resolved]

[What we're doing to prevent it from happening again]

[Offer / gesture if appropriate — credit, extension, apology]

[Direct contact for questions]

[CEO Name]

---

**Crisis communication rules:**
1. Speed matters more than perfection. A fast "we're aware and working on it" beats a perfect message 2 hours late.
2. Never speculate. Only say what you know is true.
3. Never blame others publicly — vendor, employee, or external cause.
4. Own it. "We made a mistake" earns more trust than "circumstances led to."
5. Say what you're doing, not just that you're sorry.

## Customer announcement format

**Subject:** [Benefit to customer — what's new or changing for them]

Hi [Name / Team],

**[What's new — one sentence from the customer's perspective]**

[Why this matters to them — how it makes their life better or solves a problem they have]

[What they need to do, if anything — keep it to one clear action]

[When this takes effect]

[Where to learn more or get help — one link or contact]

[CEO or team sign-off]

## Partner / vendor communication format

For issues or escalations:

Subject: [Company Name] — [Issue] — Action Required

[Name],

[State the issue directly — what happened, when, and what the impact was on your business]

[What you need from them — specific, time-bound]

[What you will do if this isn't resolved — be firm but professional]

[Contact details for follow-up]

[Your name]

## High-stakes email principles

**Opening:** Lead with the most important sentence. Don't warm up with pleasantries in business emails.

**Body:** One topic per email. If it needs more than 3 paragraphs, consider whether it should be a meeting instead.

**Ask:** Every email ends with one clear ask or next step. "Let me know your thoughts" is not an ask.

**Length:** If it takes more than 90 seconds to read, it's probably too long.

**Tone test:** Read it aloud. Would you say this to someone's face? If not, rewrite it.

## Difficult messages — how to handle them

### Layoff or restructure announcement
Structure:
1. What is happening (direct, no euphemisms)
2. Why — business reason only, honest
3. Who is affected and how they'll be supported (severance, references, timing)
4. What happens next and when
5. What this means for those who remain

Never lead with "this was the hardest decision we've ever made." Lead with the decision.

### Responding to a public complaint or negative press
- Acknowledge it without amplifying it
- Respond once, clearly, and don't get into a back-and-forth
- If there's a factual error: correct it calmly with evidence
- If the complaint is valid: own it and state what you're doing

### Saying no to a request
Structure:
- Acknowledge the request
- Give the real reason for the no (not a vague excuse)
- If possible, offer an alternative or a "not now"
- Close warmly

Example: "We can't take this on in Q2 — the team is at capacity and this would compromise quality on our current commitments. Let's revisit in Q3 when we have bandwidth."
