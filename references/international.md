# International Expansion — CEO Guide

Use this when the CEO is considering or actively expanding into new countries or regions: market entry, hiring abroad, local compliance, pricing for new markets, or managing a distributed international team.

---

## Standard output format

**International Expansion Summary — [Region / Country]**

**Headline:** [What is the expansion situation and what does it mean?]

**Market opportunity:** [Size, growth rate, competitive density — one paragraph]

**Biggest risk:** [The #1 thing that could make this expansion fail]

**Recommended approach:** [Which entry strategy and why]

**First 90-day action plan:** [3 concrete steps to validate or launch]

## Market entry strategies — choose one

### 1. Remote-first (lowest risk)
Serve customers in the new market from your existing base. Hire one local sales or CS person remotely. No legal entity.

Best for: Testing demand before committing. Works well for SaaS or digital services.
Risk: Slower trust-building. Some enterprise customers require local entity.

### 2. Hire locally, entity later
Hire 1–3 local employees as contractors or via an Employer of Record (EOR). Establish entity once revenue justifies it.

Best for: Markets where you've already won a few customers organically.
Risk: EOR costs add 20–30% to employment cost. Some IP risk without entity.

### 3. Local entity first
Register a subsidiary or branch office before hiring. Required in some heavily regulated markets.

Best for: Government contracts, financial services, healthcare, markets requiring data residency.
Risk: High setup cost ($10K–$50K+), 3–6 months to establish, ongoing compliance burden.

### 4. Partnership / reseller
Partner with a local company who knows the market. They sell, you provide the product.

Best for: Markets where relationships matter more than product, or where you lack local knowledge.
Risk: Slower growth, margin sharing (typically 20–40%), less control over customer experience.

### 5. Acquisition
Buy a local company with existing customers and team.

Best for: Fast entry with established customer base and local credibility.
Risk: High cost, integration complexity, culture clash.

## Regional guides

### South Asia (Bangladesh, India, Pakistan, Sri Lanka)

**Market characteristics:**
- Price-sensitive — enterprise deals possible but SMB volume is where growth is
- Relationship-driven sales — trust before transaction
- Long decision cycles for enterprise; fast for SMB
- Strong English proficiency (India, Bangladesh) — reduces localization burden
- Growing SaaS adoption, especially post-2020

**Entry priorities:**
- Lead with India first (largest market, most mature SaaS ecosystem)
- Bangladesh: strong for fintech, garments, and government digitization
- Local partnerships accelerate trust significantly

**Compliance:**
- India: GST registration required if selling to Indian companies above threshold (~₹20 lakh). Data localization laws evolving — check RBI guidelines for financial data
- Bangladesh: VAT registration may be required. NBR (National Board of Revenue) governs
- Pakistan: Sales tax may apply. SECP governs corporate registration

**Hiring:**
- EOR options: Deel, Remote, Papaya Global all cover India. Bangladesh options more limited
- Salary benchmarks (USD, approximate): Software engineer $12–25K/yr · Sales $8–18K/yr · Marketing $8–15K/yr
- Notice periods: typically 1–3 months for senior roles

**Cultural notes:**
- Hierarchy matters — decisions often require senior sign-off
- Direct "no" is rare — read between the lines
- Relationship investment pays off significantly in enterprise

---

### Southeast Asia (Singapore, Indonesia, Vietnam, Philippines, Thailand, Malaysia)

**Market characteristics:**
- Highly diverse — 11 countries, 600M+ people, major language differences
- Singapore: hub for regional HQ, strong rule of law, expensive
- Indonesia: largest market by population, mobile-first, complex regulatory
- Vietnam/Philippines: strong tech talent, English-speaking workforce

**Entry priorities:**
- Singapore first for B2B/enterprise (regional HQ of most multinationals)
- Indonesia for consumer or SMB scale
- Vietnam and Philippines for talent and cost-effective team expansion

**Compliance:**
- Singapore: easiest to incorporate in Asia (1–3 days), GST applies above S$1M revenue
- Indonesia: requires local entity for most business activities; complex tax
- Vietnam: foreign investment regulations apply — partner structure often needed

**Hiring:**
- Singapore: expensive ($60–100K USD for senior roles)
- Vietnam/Philippines: competitive talent at $15–40K USD

---

### Middle East (UAE, Saudi Arabia, Qatar, Egypt)

**Market characteristics:**
- Government and enterprise are the biggest buyers
- Relationship and reputation are critical — referrals open more doors than cold outreach
- Strong appetite for digital transformation, especially post-Vision 2030 (Saudi)
- Data sovereignty requirements increasingly common

**Entry priorities:**
- UAE (Dubai/Abu Dhabi) first — most open market, strong English, gateway to region
- Saudi Arabia: largest economy, government is a major buyer, but slower and more complex
- Egypt: growing tech sector, more price-sensitive

**Compliance:**
- UAE: Free Zone entity is fastest and most flexible for foreign companies. Mainland entity needed to sell directly to government
- Saudi Arabia: VAT at 15%. CITC (tech regulator) governs some sectors. Data localization requirements for government
- Qatar: similarly structured to UAE

**Cultural notes:**
- Ramadan significantly slows business activity — plan around it
- Friday is a weekend day in most of the Gulf
- Face-to-face meetings are essential for enterprise deals — budget for travel

---

### UK and Europe

**Market characteristics:**
- UK: familiar for English-speaking companies, post-Brexit complications for EU sales
- Europe: high GDPR compliance burden but strong enterprise spending
- Germany: largest economy, risk-averse buyers, long sales cycles, strong preference for local entities
- France: government/enterprise strong, French language preferred for public sector

**Compliance:**
- GDPR applies to any EU customer data — non-negotiable
- UK GDPR (post-Brexit) mirrors EU GDPR but is separate
- VAT registration required in most EU countries once crossing thresholds
- Employment law strongly favors employees — termination is expensive and process-heavy

**Hiring:**
- UK: employment contracts must comply with Working Time Regulations, statutory redundancy pay applies
- Germany: works councils (Betriebsrat) have co-determination rights in companies with 5+ employees
- France: 35-hour work week, significant notice periods and severance requirements

## Employer of Record (EOR) — when to use one

An EOR lets you hire employees in a country without setting up a legal entity. The EOR is the legal employer; you manage the work.

**Use EOR when:**
- Hiring 1–5 people in a new market to test it
- You're not sure you'll stay in the market long-term
- You want to move fast without 3–6 months of entity setup

**Popular EOR providers:** Deel · Remote · Rippling Global · Papaya Global · Velocity Global

**Cost:** EOR adds 15–30% on top of salary and benefits. Worth it for speed; expensive at scale (10+ employees → get your own entity).

## International pricing strategy

**Don't just convert USD prices.** Local pricing should reflect:
- Local purchasing power (use BigMac Index or GDP per capita as a rough proxy)
- Competitive pricing in that market
- What the customer's ROI looks like in local currency

**Common approaches:**
- **Flat global pricing** — simple, but overprices low-income markets and underprices high-income ones
- **Regional pricing tiers** — e.g., 40% discount for South Asia, 20% for Southeast Asia vs US pricing
- **Local currency billing** — reduces friction, increases conversion, adds FX risk

**Rule of thumb:** If your US price is $500/month, your South Asia price might be $150–200/month and still represent similar value to the customer.

## International expansion red flags

- Entering a new market without a local champion (employee or partner who knows the terrain)
- Assuming what works in your home market works elsewhere — product, pricing, and messaging all need adaptation
- Underestimating compliance and legal setup time — always takes 2x longer than expected
- Hiring remotely in a country without understanding local employment law
- No dedicated budget for travel — relationships in most international markets require in-person visits
- Expanding to 3+ markets simultaneously — dilutes focus and resources

---

## CEO questions before committing to a market

1. Do we already have customers or inbound leads from this market?
2. Who is our local champion — do we know anyone who can open doors?
3. What would success look like in 12 months — and what's the minimum investment to find out?
4. What compliance or legal requirements could block us?
5. Can we serve this market from our current base before committing to a local hire or entity?
