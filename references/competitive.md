# Competitive Intelligence — CEO Guide

Use this when the CEO needs to understand the competitive landscape, track what competitors are doing, structure win/loss analysis, sharpen positioning, or prepare for a competitive sales situation.

---

## Standard output format

**Competitive Summary — [Competitor / Market]**

**Headline:** [One sentence — what is the competitive situation and what does it mean for us?]

**Our position:** [Are we winning, losing, or holding ground in competitive deals?]

**Biggest competitive threat right now:** [Specific competitor, what they're doing, why it matters]

**Recommended action:** [One thing the CEO should do or decide based on this intelligence]

---

## Competitive landscape map

When asked to map the competitive landscape, structure it as:

### Direct competitors
Companies solving the same problem for the same customer.

| Competitor | Strength | Weakness | Our win rate vs them | Trend |
|-----------|----------|----------|---------------------|-------|
| [Name] | [1 strength] | [1 weakness] | X% | Growing / Flat / Declining |

### Indirect competitors
Alternative solutions (different approach, same problem — e.g., customers could use spreadsheets instead of your tool).

| Alternative | Why customers choose it | Why they switch to us |
|-------------|------------------------|----------------------|
| [Name] | [Reason] | [Trigger] |

### Emerging threats
Companies that don't compete today but could in 12–18 months.

| Company | What they're building | Why they're a risk | Timeline |
|---------|----------------------|-------------------|----------|
| [Name] | [Product direction] | [Overlap risk] | [When] |

## Win/loss analysis framework

Run this quarterly. Analyze the last 20–30 closed deals.

### Data to collect per deal
- Did we win or lose?
- Who did we lose to (or who did we beat)?
- What was the deal size and customer segment?
- What was the primary reason stated for the decision?
- What was the real reason (if different)?

### Win/loss summary format

**Wins — why we won (this quarter)**
1. [Reason] — appeared in X% of wins
2. [Reason] — appeared in X% of wins
3. [Reason] — appeared in X% of wins

**Losses — why we lost**
1. [Reason] — appeared in X% of losses → Lost most to [Competitor]
2. [Reason] — appeared in X% of losses
3. [Reason] — appeared in X% of losses

**Pattern:** [What is the single clearest insight from this analysis?]

**Action:** [What should change in our sales motion, product, or positioning?]

## Competitive monitoring — what to track

Set up monitoring for each major competitor. Check weekly or bi-weekly:

| Signal | Where to check | What to look for |
|--------|---------------|-----------------|
| Product updates | Their changelog, blog, release notes | New features that close gaps with you |
| Pricing changes | Their pricing page | Moving upmarket, downmarket, or new tiers |
| Hiring | LinkedIn job posts | What roles tell you where they're investing |
| Customer reviews | G2, Trustpilot, Reddit | What customers love and hate |
| Press coverage | Google Alerts | Funding, partnerships, leadership changes |
| Social signals | LinkedIn, Twitter | Executive posts signal strategic direction |
| Job listings | Their careers page | Engineering, sales, and marketing hires signal priorities |

**CEO rule:** A competitor hiring 10 enterprise AEs is a clearer signal than a press release.

## Competitive positioning — sharpening your edge

### The positioning test
For your product, complete this sentence:

> "For [target customer], [Company Name] is the only [category] that [unique benefit] because [proof point]."

If you can't complete it clearly, your positioning needs work.

### How to find your genuine differentiation
Ask your best customers: "Why did you choose us over [competitor]?"
Ask customers who churned: "What made you switch?"
Ask deals you lost: "What would we have needed to be different?"

The truth is in those answers — not in your internal positioning doc.

### Common positioning mistakes
- "We're better" — not specific enough
- "We're easier to use" — every competitor says this
- "We have more features" — features are copied; outcomes are not
- Positioning against a competitor rather than for a customer outcome

### The contrast frame
Instead of "We're better than [Competitor]", say:
"[Competitor] is built for [X type of company]. We're built for [Y type of company] who needs [specific outcome]."

This works because it's honest and helps prospects self-select.

## Competitive battle cards — quick format

One card per major competitor. Sales team uses these in live deals.

---

**Battle Card: vs [Competitor]**

**When you hear this:** "We're also looking at [Competitor]"

**Their pitch:** [How they describe themselves]

**Their real strengths:** [Be honest — 2 things they genuinely do well]

**Their real weaknesses:** [2 things customers actually complain about]

**Our strengths vs them:** [2–3 things we do better for the right customer]

**The trap to avoid:** [The argument that sounds good but doesn't land — don't use it]

**Proof point:** "[Customer name] switched from [Competitor] because [specific reason] and achieved [specific outcome]."

**If they bring up pricing:** "[Our price] vs [their price] — the difference is [X]. Here's the ROI calculation for a company your size: [number]."

## Red flags in competitive positioning

- Sales team doesn't know how to handle the top 3 competitor objections
- Win rate against a specific competitor declining 3+ months in a row
- A competitor closing deals 50%+ faster than you (your sales cycle may be the problem)
- Customers saying competitors have a feature you don't — appearing in multiple deals
- Positioning that sounds identical to your competitors' positioning
- No structured process for capturing why deals are won or lost
