# Fundraising Playbook

## When the CEO says: "we're thinking about raising", "how do we raise a Series A", "prep me for investor meetings", "review this term sheet", "what's our raise readiness"

---

## Step 1 — Assess raise readiness before anything else

Before approaching investors, confirm these are true:

| Signal | Pre-Seed | Seed | Series A | Series B |
|--------|----------|------|----------|----------|
| MRR | Idea / MVP | $10K–$50K | $100K–$500K | $500K+ |
| Growth MoM | N/A | 10–15% | 15–20% | 15%+ |
| Customers | Early users | 10–50 paying | 50–200 | 200+ |
| Retention | N/A | Early signal | NRR >100% | NRR >110% |
| Team | Founders | 3–8 | 10–25 | 25–75 |
| Use of funds | Clear | Product + GTM | Scale GTM | Expand |

**Red flag:** Raising too early costs equity and credibility. If metrics don't match the stage, fix the metrics before raising — don't raise to fix them.

---

## Step 2 — Build the raise narrative

Every raise needs a clear story. Use this structure:

### The one-paragraph raise story
> "[Company] is [what you do] for [who]. We've grown from [X] to [Y MRR] in [Z months] by [key insight about why you win]. We're raising [$X] to [specific use of funds] which will get us to [milestone] in [timeframe]. That milestone sets up [next round / path to profitability]."

### What investors are actually evaluating
1. **Market** — Is this a big enough market to return the fund?
2. **Traction** — Are the numbers real and growing?
3. **Team** — Can this team execute and adapt?
4. **Product** — Is there genuine differentiation?
5. **Unit economics** — Does the business model work at scale?

---

## Step 3 — Build the investor target list

### Tier your list

**Tier 1 — Dream investors (5–10)**
- Strong fit with your sector and stage
- Portfolio companies you've researched and can reference
- Warm intro required — don't cold email Tier 1

**Tier 2 — Good fit (15–20)**
- Sector fit, some portfolio overlap
- Warm intro preferred but cold works with strong personalisation

**Tier 3 — Reach / practice (10–15)**
- Less obvious fit
- Use for practice pitches and referral potential

### Where to find investors
- Crunchbase — filter by stage, sector, recent investments
- Visible.vc investor database
- AngelList (pre-seed/seed)
- LinkedIn — search "[sector] investor [stage]"
- Portfolio pages of funds you admire
- Your existing investors and advisors — ask for 3 intros each

### Warm intro path
1. Map second-degree connections to target investors on LinkedIn
2. Ask the mutual connection: "Would you be comfortable making an intro to [investor]? Happy to send you a forwardable email."
3. Send a forwardable email: 3 sentences, one key metric, link to deck

## Step 4 — Build the pitch deck

### Standard 10-slide structure

| Slide | Content |
|-------|---------|
| 1. Cover | Company name, one-line description, raise amount |
| 2. Problem | Specific pain, evidence it's real, current workarounds fail |
| 3. Solution | What you built, why it works, demo or screenshot |
| 4. Market | TAM/SAM/SOM — show the math, not just big numbers |
| 5. Traction | MRR growth chart, key metrics, notable customers |
| 6. Business model | How you make money, unit economics (CAC, LTV, payback) |
| 7. Go-to-market | How you acquire customers, what's working, what's next |
| 8. Competition | Honest map — where you win and why |
| 9. Team | Why this team, relevant experience, key hires planned |
| 10. Ask | Amount, use of funds (specific %), milestones it buys |

### Common deck mistakes
- Slides 1–3 are too slow — investors decide in the first 2 minutes
- Market slide uses top-down TAM with no bottom-up validation
- No MRR growth chart — this is the most important slide at seed and beyond
- Competition slide dismisses competitors — shows naivety
- Ask slide vague ("scale the team") — be specific about milestones

## Step 5 — Run the process

### Timeline (typical)
- **Week 1–2:** Finalise deck, data room, target list
- **Week 2–4:** First-round outreach, intro calls (30 min, no deck)
- **Week 3–6:** Partner meetings (60 min, with deck)
- **Week 4–8:** Due diligence, reference calls, term sheet
- **Week 6–12:** Term sheet negotiation, legal, close

### Managing the process
- Run all conversations in parallel — never serial
- Create urgency by setting a timeline: "We're targeting to close by [date]"
- Track every investor in a pipeline (Notion, Sheets, or Airtable): Stage → Outreach → Call → Partner Meeting → DD → Term Sheet → Closed
- Follow up every 5–7 days with a progress update if no response

### Investor meeting format
- **First call (30 min):** CEO tells the story, investor asks questions. Goal: get to partner meeting.
- **Partner meeting (60 min):** Full pitch, deeper questions, team assessment. Goal: get to term sheet.
- **Diligence calls:** Reference checks on you, customer calls, financial review. Goal: close.

## Step 6 — Data room checklist

Have these ready before first partner meetings:

**Company**
- [ ] Certificate of incorporation
- [ ] Cap table (fully diluted)
- [ ] Shareholder agreements
- [ ] Board minutes (last 12 months)

**Financials**
- [ ] P&L (last 12–24 months)
- [ ] Balance sheet
- [ ] Cash flow statement
- [ ] Monthly MRR/ARR breakdown
- [ ] Unit economics model (CAC, LTV, payback)
- [ ] 18–24 month financial model with assumptions

**Product & customers**
- [ ] Product demo or walkthrough
- [ ] Customer list (with ARR per customer if comfortable)
- [ ] Churn data and explanation
- [ ] NPS or customer satisfaction data
- [ ] 2–3 customer reference contacts

**Team**
- [ ] Org chart
- [ ] Key employee agreements
- [ ] Option pool breakdown

## Step 7 — Term sheet negotiation

### Key terms to understand and negotiate

| Term | What it means | What to watch |
|------|--------------|---------------|
| **Valuation (pre-money)** | Company value before investment | Negotiate — don't accept first offer |
| **Investment amount** | How much they're putting in | Confirm this is enough runway for milestones |
| **Liquidation preference** | Who gets paid first in exit | 1x non-participating is standard; push back on 2x or participating |
| **Anti-dilution** | Protection if you raise at lower valuation | Broad-based weighted average is standard; full ratchet is aggressive |
| **Board seats** | Who controls the board | Founders should retain majority at seed |
| **Pro-rata rights** | Right to invest in future rounds | Standard — not a major negotiation point |
| **Option pool** | Employee equity reserve | Often required pre-money — negotiate size (10–15% is standard) |
| **Vesting** | When founders' shares are earned | 4 years with 1-year cliff is standard |
| **Exclusivity / No-shop** | Period where you can't talk to other investors | Limit to 30 days maximum |
| **Information rights** | What financials you share | Monthly or quarterly financials is standard |

### 🔴 Red flags in term sheets
- Participating liquidation preference (investors get preference AND pro-rata on exit)
- Full ratchet anti-dilution (very investor-friendly, punishing for founders)
- Redemption rights (investor can demand their money back after X years)
- Supermajority voting on routine decisions
- Board control at seed stage

### Negotiation approach
- Never negotiate against yourself — let them move first
- Prioritise valuation and board control; let minor terms go
- Get legal counsel who specialises in startup term sheets
- Talk to 2–3 founders who've taken money from this investor before signing

## Step 8 — Common fundraising mistakes

| Mistake | What to do instead |
|---------|-------------------|
| Raising when desperate (< 3 months runway) | Start raise at 12–18 months runway |
| Pitching without warm intros | Build relationships 6 months before you need them |
| Showing hockey stick projections with no basis | Show conservative model with clear assumptions |
| Underestimating time to close | Plan for 4–6 months from first call to money in bank |
| Taking the first term sheet without shopping | Always have at least 2 competing term sheets |
| Giving board control too early | Retain majority until Series B+ |
| Not checking investor references | Always call founders from their portfolio — both successes and failures |
| Raising too much | Extra capital = extra dilution + pressure to deploy fast |

---

## Red flags — trigger 🔴 Alert

- Cash runway under 6 months when not yet in active raise process → 🔴 Start fundraising now — you have no leverage below 6 months
- Approaching investors without a data room ready → 🔴 Investors will request data room within first 2 meetings — not having one signals unpreparedness
- Single customer > 30% of ARR during raise → 🔴 Concentration risk will be raised in every partner meeting — address it proactively in the deck
- No MRR growth for 3+ months before raise → 🔴 Flat growth kills raise momentum — defer until growth resumes or have a clear explanation
